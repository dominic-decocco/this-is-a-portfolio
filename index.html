Tumpero

go to google store
In the last year of college, my flatmate and I started a website gathering all information about our city's restaurant and bars, always highlighting the discounts: June 16th, 2016 Tumpero was live as the first gastronomic city guide. The restaurants would pay a monthly fee in order to be listed and advertised.

As the user base and affiliated establishments grew quickly, we had to shift to a mobile app in only 6 months

1/5 • Tumpero's Logo and In-App screenshot (Tumpero is a variation af the word 'seasoning' in Portuguese)
A quick and seamless way to make that transition was to use a platform called GoodBarber to create the App. Even though it delivered what we needed to start, our new ideas became limited by the platform's features.

I had to reach for HTML, CSS and JS in order to tweak and improve Tumpero the way we wanted. A couple of months later, I was splitting my work hours into graphic design and front-end development

2/5 • List of restaurants
I was in charge of the creative process and its implementation through coding. The branding, logo, the design behind the app and social media content were all under my umbrella.

However, my absolute passion was the poster design for some of our campaigns. The freedom to choose any creative direction and later seeing a huge print of your work on the street is just incredibly satisfying to me

3/5 • The deals could be sorted by day of the week
At the end of 2017 - with 4 team members - we reached the mark of 100 establishments signed to the App and the data feedback continued to surpass our expectations:

In December we had 15,772 sessions, 102,659 pageviews and 1,732 downloads with an average visit duration of 4 minutes.

4/5 • Monthly data report
Although we've always had big plans for Tumpero, in 2018 our priorities became to shift towards moving to Europe, so we had to discontinue the project in September that year.

To this day, I can honestly say that Tumpero is my proudest achievement. The financial return of doing what you love was only smaller than the experience earned by fighting for what you believe in.

Portfolio

go to code
I did it my way:

To a graphic designer, Portfolio is key. To a graphic designer slash front-end developer, the website is the portfolio.

My first thoughts of showcasing the work on Behance were quickly erased by the necessity to make this project as personal as a tooth brush: 100% ahead of the art direction, design process and coding (except for one question on Stackoverflow)

1/4
Second guessing the stroke weight:

Honestly, the biggest challlenge I faced was to not have a deadline or client approval, which made me prolong the creation process on Illustrator.

Inspiration came from 2 places: the colors and typography from Toy Fight and low poly/3d concept from Plink. Once the low poly aesthetic was choosen, the portfolio was put together in a couple of weeks

2/4
Box layout is not my cup of tea:

As it's clear on most of my websites, I like to create sections that bleed into the next, so the whole project would tell a story and feel like a natural flow, compelling the user to keep scrolling. I'll separate content into boxes when the client is the government.

The whole portfolio was made on a single HTML file, for two reasons: as a "story being told", I didn't want the user to step out of it by loading another page and also, there's not that much content to fill two pages

3/4
Nerding out a bit:

As this portfolio is basically one big illustration, the hardest part was to create, size and position every single element to fit their specific part and, at the same time, be responsive with only one media query: 'To use pixels or viewports? That's the question'.

For the image sliders I reached for a little bit of logic on Jquery and created a couple of handlers, no biggy.
Some vanilla JS to trigger section openings and parallax effect for the meteors to roll cool

4/4
